Sumobot competitions typically feature robots (either player or autonomously controlled) aiming to push the opponent out of a designated ring. Designs are usually within given limits for weight, size, etc.

The sumobot chassis is created from 20-gauge sheet metal, mounted onto a provided robot body. Components are soldered to a wired controller using metal tabs to control forward / reverse direction of the wheels. The bot can be powered by an external power source wired to the controller, or rechargeable batteries.

Note: Original design had a hinged triangular plow which could drop down to extend push range while remaining in size restrictions.

Front of the robot.

Rear of the robot.

Overhead view of the robot.

Requirements:
  • Fits within a 12cm x 12cm x 12cm space.
  • Maximum weight of 500g.
  • Chassis designed by parts available in the metalwork shop.
Design Details:
  • Created from 20-Gauge Steel.
    -Body cut to form, bent and MIG welded.
  • Front plow was originally a 6cm pyramidal plow mounted to the front by a hinge, allowing for bot to remain within restricted dimensions.
    -Plow would drop upon bot movement, allowing hands-free deployment.
    -Ramp shape would aid in flipping opponents sideways.
  • Rear plow mounted closer to the wheels for increased power.
    -Allows for viable reverse controlling, covering an additional angle of attack.
  • Wheel guards created to avoid opponent plates from getting underneath.
Details of note:
  • Sumobot won the competition between 60 participants in best-of-3 tournament style matchups.
  • Awarded Top Design by peers.

Metalwork has always been a huge passion of mine, as it is an excellent medium to express creative freedom, allowing one to create sculptures, replicas, and usable tools from a workable and durable material. This has allowed me to learn the various shop machines as well as proper design methods and welding.

This project was a capstone for my senior year in our high-school automotive shop. A friend and I opted to create a wearable replica of the Wakandan Shield given to Captain America in Avengers: Infinity War. The shield was first measured and created out of paper, before being transferred to cardboard for dimensioning and fitting. Finally, it was cut from 20-gauge sheet metal, welded, and painted to prevent external wear.

    -Forearm strap secured by cutting up an old belt and riveting into place.
  • Padded using fabric from an old chair and crimped / hot glued into place.
  • Sprayed initially with automotive paint before a second pass of clear primer.
    -Primer protects the parts not painted black while preserving the detail work.
    -Automotive paint used for corrosion resistance and scratch protection.

The Maasai are a semi-nomadic tribe in Eastern Africa. Due to this lifestyle, their belongings are to be portable and light-weight. This project was to design a cardboard chair for the Massai schoolchildren which should be sustainable, easy to construct, lightweight and compact for ease of transportation.

Our team designed the chair with an interlocking grid surrounded tightly by a cylindrical sheath. The lid sits atop the grid with a slot in the back for a back rest. The chair can be unfolded quickly, and when packed up, can be fitted within the cylindrical sheath. The entire assembly can fold flat and be held compact with string.

A first-year engineering project is to create a robot claw which can be operated remotely or autonomously. The given task was split into two categories: The first round consists of picking up an array of objects varying in size, shape, and weight. The second round would test the claw's ability to pick up objects in bulk.

The claw body was created from aluminum sheet metal and held together by rivets. The assembly was operated by a servo motor controlled by an Arduino Uno. This was all powered by an on-board rechargeable power bank. The whole robot would be lowered on a string by the controller, where a sonar module would detect the floor and automatically close the jaws of the claw.

Claw front-side orthographic view (Units in cm).

Claw right-side orthographic view (Units in cm).

Framework of the claw without any electrical components (parts had to be returned to the professors).

Completed claw with all the electrical components.

Requirements (As decided by the team).
  • Be constructed from the aluminum sheet metal (This was an exercise for familiarization with the metal working tools).
  • Pick up varying types of objects (flat, round, small, etc).
  • Have a quick reaction time.
    -Detect when near the floor and pick up object(s) without a long wait. -Keep closed for adequate time to get to drop-off zone (~10s).
Design Details
  • Coded in the Arduino Ide (C++) and run on the Arduino Uno using provided parts.
    -Sonar module allows for the claw to autonomously retrieve and release objects.
    -Battery pack provides 4+ hours of use before needing to recharge (via micro-usb).
  • Motor rotating in one direction would pull a line on the outside to open the claw. Rotating in the opposite direction would loosen the outside and pull a line on the inside to close.
  • One hinged jaw similar to a broom and dustpan.
    -Utilizing full range of motion of the motor ensured a tight clamp on objects.
    -Allowed for less moving parts.
  • Folded side tabs angled outward to create a scoop shape and prevent objects from falling out the edges.

A second year introductory project: Create a line following robot using given components and household materials. The robot will be given a series of courses to run scaling in difficulty. Projects would be assessed through various criterion such as design, overall neatness, code, and both difficulty of course, and time required to complete said course.

This Line-Following robot utilizes phototransistors to detect a black line on white paper. Using logic coded in the Arduino IDE, it is able to trace the line including around bends and over gaps. Bot body was created using common materials such as cardboard, paper, and the all-important engineering material: duct-tape. Utilizing a lofted design, the components were kept open instead of having a chassis to give a unique and steampunk appeal.

You can view the detailed description sheet I wrote for the project as a pdf here.

Or view the project code on GitHub

Overhead view of the robot. Arduino, Breadboard, and wires for each phototransistor are found up top.

Underside view of robot. Sensor bracket can be seen on the left. Individual motors powered each wheel.

Inside of the robot. Batteries and H-Bridge are kept underneath the loft as they required less tinkering.

Shot of the cardboard sensor bracket. phototransistors mounted underneath.

Completed robot.

Requirements
  • Be made from household materials (Parts from given kit were allowed)
  • Must be autonomously controlled
Design Details
  • Five phototransistors on the sensor bracket detect the black line against the white background.
  • Code runs off an Arduino Uno R3.
    -Logic aims to keep the middle three sensors on the line.
    -When an outer sensor detects black, rotate the robot in that direction.
    -If both sensors detect lines, or all sensors do not detect a line, move straight forward until line is found.
  • Lofted platform keeps components organized and accessible.
    -Wiring and Arduino are kept on the raised level for ease of modification.
    -Power source and H-Bridge kept on lower level as they don't require as much attention.
  • Powered by two 9V batteries wired in series.
    -Bot can still run with one 9V, however it loses the ability to fine-tune motor speed.

A second year capstone project. Modern audio synthesizers are typically distanced from the creator: utilizing an array of knobs, buttons, and switches on boards to achieve the end result. This can often be unintuitive on what mechanisms control which aspects of audio, and create confusion for new and inexperienced users. To this end, our team opted to create a device which integrates the users hand range of motion to produce these same results with a more innovative and intuitive design.

The Audio Synthesizer Glove is capable of translating hand movements such as finger displacement, hand rotation and gestures into tangible sound. All housings and splints were CAD modelled in SolidWorks and 3-D printed, with a combination of flex sensors and accelerometer used to create integrated input methods. Main components were soldered together, and communication was achieved over WiFi through the use of an Espressif ESP-32 module. The user interface leverages a JavaScript library (Tone.js) to create audio ranging from drum beats to notes produced by traditional synthesizers and oscillators.

-As the glove isn't included (obviously), the interface has been provided with sliders for user interaction.

Design Details
  • Utilizes finger movement and hand gestures to navigate and use the workspace.
    -Flex sensors on the thumb, pointer, and middle fingers control the volume, pitch (hz) and cadence (bpm) respectively.

  • Able to emulate audio through a variety of drum beats, synthesizers and oscillators.
    -Tracks currently include a kick and snare drum, 5 synthesizers covering quarter, half, and whole notes, and two oscillators for square and sine waves.

The ability to make subtle and deliberate movements in day to day life is often overlooked. For many afflicted with hand tremors (such as those with severe anxiety or Parkinson's Disease) suffer from hand tremors which can complicate simple tasks such as drinking from a cup or writing with a pen.

My team's plan is to create a product which combines electromyography alongside accelerometer and gyrometer sensors to monitor the hand motions of the user. A machine learning algorithm is to analyze these electrical signals and determine whether a movement is voluntary or involuntary (tremoring). The device in turn will mitigate or reduce these tremors mechanically through the use of angular momentum produced by a flywheel mounted on the rear of the palm. The motivation is to have an end product which can easily be worn and taken off without impeding the everyday actions of the user while actively reducing the intensity of their hand tremors.

Proposed Requirements
  • Be non-intrusive to daily activity. (eg. Limiting movement, causing injury)
  • Components should be locally located on the user. (No tether or wires)
    -This means any code must be loaded onto the microcontroller or an external memory card.
  • Machine learning algorithm should be able to accurately discern the difference between voluntary and involuntary movement.
  • Have a long lasting (rechargeable) battery.
Planned Details
  • Use Electromyography (EMG) sensors to detect electrical impulses in the users arms.
  • Have a new user perform tasks which may induce shaking in the hands in order to have training data.
    -Sensor data will be saved to an external SD card to reduce on active memory usage.
    -Machine learning algorithm will then be changed based on the collected data to determine when tremor occurs, the intensity of tremor, and the movement of the user.
  • Outputs of the algorithm will be able to:
    -Isolate intensity of tremor from noise due to movements.
    -Correlate intensity of tremor with accelerometer data to determine what motor speed is required to mitigate tremor.
  • Create testing rig to remove human error; test the effective force produced at a given rpm of flywheel.
  • More details to be determined as project progresses
  • Find necessary components and method of attaching to the arm.
    -Arduino Nano 33 BLE Microcontroller was selected for its computational specs and the integrated 9-axis accelerometer/gyrometer/magnetometer.
    -Brushless DC motor used alongside a 3D printed flywheel will be used to produce a torque through angular mmomentum.
  • Learn Machine Learning in Python.
    -Coding was broken down into three seperate algorithms:
    -Determine if the user is moving their hand / arm.
    -Determine if the user is tremoring by isolating the noise from motions.
    -Calculate the appropriate motor speed required to produce adequate force.
  • Design a testing rig to obtain accurate mitigation force.
    -Scotch Yoke design was used to produce an oscillatory vertical motion which could be adjusted in terms of frequency and amplitude.
    -Flywheel induced force measured using a protractor and springs.
    -Use of external rig eliminates potential human error and produces more accurate correlation between amplitude and rpm.

Diving equipment can often be a lot of multitasking for divers -having to keep up with time spent underwater, checking oxygen levels, and ensuring safety when resurfacing (the bends, nitrogen narcosis, etc).

A set diving goggles with an inbuilt display to show all necessary information for divers (O2 levels, depth, time spent underwater, pressure). This would help alleviate much of the required juggling when diving allowing the user ot remain on task. External pressure sensors relay pressure, depth, and temperature. Integrated stopwatch could keep track of time spent at depth, as well as provide timing when resurfacing. On-board components are to be encased in a pressure proof container to prevent water / pressure damage.

Proposed Requirements
  • Displays all information on a Heads-Up-Display (HUD).
  • Survive a crush depth of ~200m (20 Bar).
  • Goggles should be drop / shock proof.
  • Corrosion Resistant.
  • Long lasting battery life.
    -Minimum 4h operable time.
    -Rechargeable.
Planned Details
  • Provides External data.
    -Time of day (24H time).
    -Current diving depth.
    -Experienced external pressure.
  • Display instrument data.
    -Goggle battery level.
